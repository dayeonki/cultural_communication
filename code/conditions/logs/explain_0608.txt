Derangements are a concept from **combinatorics**, a branch of mathematics focused on counting and arrangements.

---

### üìå What is a Derangement?

A **derangement** is a **permutation** of a set where **no element appears in its original position**.

#### Example:
Suppose we have 3 elements:  
\[ A = \{1, 2, 3\} \]

All permutations of A:
1. (1, 2, 3) ‚ùå ‚Äî elements are in original positions
2. (1, 3, 2) ‚ùå ‚Äî 1 is still in place
3. (2, 1, 3) ‚ùå ‚Äî 3 is still in place
4. (2, 3, 1) ‚úÖ ‚Äî derangement
5. (3, 1, 2) ‚úÖ ‚Äî derangement
6. (3, 2, 1) ‚ùå ‚Äî 2 is still in place

So, there are **2 derangements** of 3 elements.

---

### üî¢ Notation & Formula

The number of derangements of \( n \) elements is denoted by:

\[
!n
\]

Derangement formula:
\[
!n = n! \left(1 - \frac{1}{1!} + \frac{1}{2!} - \frac{1}{3!} + \dots + (-1)^n \frac{1}{n!} \right)
\]

Or approximately:
\[
!n \approx \frac{n!}{e}
\]
where \( e \approx 2.71828 \)

---

### üî¢ Example Calculations:

- \( !1 = 0 \)
- \( !2 = 1 \)
- \( !3 = 2 \)
- \( !4 = 9 \)
- \( !5 = 44 \)

---

### üí° Application:
Derangements are useful in problems like:

- The **"hat-check problem"**: If \( n \) guests check their hats and hats are returned at random, how many ways are there such that **no guest gets their own hat**?

**Hyperlexia** is a condition characterized by an early and intense ability to read, often well beyond the expected age level, but typically accompanied by difficulties with language comprehension and social communication. It is most commonly observed in young children.

### Key Features of Hyperlexia:

1. **Precocious Reading Ability**:
   - Children with hyperlexia often learn to read on their own by age 2 or 3, without formal teaching.
   - They may decode written words with remarkable accuracy, even before they understand their meaning.

   - They may have trouble with answering questions, following verbal instructions, or holding typical conversations.

3. **Social Challenges**:
   - Many children with hyperlexia show behaviors similar to those on the autism spectrum, such as limited eye contact, repetitive behaviors, and a preference for routines.
   - They may have trouble understanding social cues or engaging in typical peer interactions.

   - Often have excellent memory for numbers, letters, and visual details.
   - May become intensely focused on letterforms, numbers, maps, or logos.

1. **Hyperlexia Type 1**: 
   - Neurotypical children who are early readers but develop normally in other areas.
   
2. **Hyperlexia Type 2**:
   - Children on the autism spectrum who also have advanced reading skills (common in some children with ASD).
   
3. **Hyperlexia Type 3**:
   - Children who display temporary autistic-like behaviors and language delays but are not on the autism spectrum. As they develop, these difficulties lessen or disappear.
